Personal Data Science/Research Projects

Expected goals models for NHL shot data

An exploration of how various conditions influence the outcomes of NHL shots, leading to a predictive model of shot outcomes. Over 1,000,000 shot records are studied in a data set spanning 10 NHL seasons. Contour maps of shot events (seen on the left) are available for various conditions.

By identifying key conditions, models based on a simple ratio are able to predict the total number of shots in a given season to within ~5-10%.

Map of transit accessibility in Hamilton, Ontario

A map of walkable accessibility for the Hamilton Street Railway (HSR) public transit network. For over 60,000 buildings in the city, the number of buses arriving with walking distance is calculated, achieving a "spatial resolution" of < 100 metres in this metric along the entirety of the HSR network.

The calculations are based on algorithm from the QGIS python module, and the webmap was made in the desktop QGIS application.

The increase in U.S.A. wildfire activity from 1992-2020

An investigation of wildfire activity in the U.S.A based on a public dataset of 2.3 million wildfires. Using standard statistical techniques, a cyclical, slowly increasing prevalence in wildfire activity overtime is quantified. The spatial distribution of wildfires is also explored, identifying the most heavily affected areas.

Ph.D. Research Projects

The effects of grain size distribution on asteroid formation (pre-print)

An investigation of the effects of modelling a distribution of dust grain sizes during the formation of asteroids via the streaming instability (SI, see "varaibility" project below). In nature, asteroid-sized objects are believed to form directly from solid material similar in size (and to a lesser extent, chemical composition) to grains of sand on Earth. This work is the first to model a realistic distribution of grain sizes in 3D simulations, and demonstrates that only grains within a narrow range of sizes participate strongly in asteroid formation.

Variability in the streaming instability (SI) (pre-print)

An investigation of the variability in a well-studied phenomonon known as the streaming instability (SI), which is believed to contribute substantially to the formation of asteroid-sized objects in young (< 10 Myr), actively-forming solar systems—i.e. protoplanetary disks. This work presents novel variations on literature-standard simulations which highlight and quantify the instrinsic variability present in this highly non-linear process.
